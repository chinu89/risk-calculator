How the Assessment Engine Works
Understanding how your inputs produce the final risk score
The calculator replicates RadarFirst's breach assessment logic — reverse-engineered from 54 verified test cases. It uses a priority-ordered decision tree, not a weighted average. One dominant factor can override all others. The key question Radar is answering is: given what we now know happened, how much harm actually occurred?
1
Mitigation is checked first — and it dominates everything
The system starts with what happened after the breach, not the breach itself. If there is forensic confirmation the data was never accessed, or it was recovered from backup, the result is LOW regardless of all other inputs — even if it was plain-text data stolen by a hacker. These are called "confirmed safe" mitigations.

Assurance-based mitigations (written agreements, policy actions) are weaker — they suggest containment was attempted, not confirmed. These usually produce MODERATE, but escalate for dangerous recipients.
2
Protection level determines whether data was readable
If the data was statistically de-identified, the risk is inherently low — no individual can be identified even if the data was accessed. This produces LOW in most cases, with only narrow exceptions for confirmed ransomware or hacker/media recipients with malicious intent.

All other protection levels (redacted, encrypted, plain text) are treated as "readable" — the exact level matters less than whether it crossed into the hands of the wrong recipient.
3
For readable data with insufficient controls, recipient and outcome determine severity
If readable data reached someone with insufficient risk controls, the recipient type is decisive. Any unauthorized recipient — vendor, hacker, member of public — always produces EXTREME. Authorized recipients (covered entities, business associates, federal agencies) are held to statutory obligations, so severity depends on what the mitigation tells us actually happened:
EXTREME
Confirmed misuse, published data, unknown outcome, or SSN exposed
HIGH
Data confirmed accessed or acquired; unable to retrieve
MODERATE
Destroyed but backup status unknown — genuine uncertainty
LOW
Forensic or physical confirmation — data provably not accessed
4
Intent and recipient type affect assurance-level outcomes
When the mitigation is assurance-based (a promise or written agreement) and the data is readable, the recipient's nature matters. Hackers and media outlets cannot be relied on to honour any assurance — so risk escalates to EXTREME if the breach was malicious, HIGH if unintentional. Other unauthorized recipients with malicious intent also escalate to HIGH. Authorized recipients with legal obligations stay at MODERATE — their statutory duties provide meaningful containment.
About this tool: This calculator was built by reverse-engineering RadarFirst through 54 empirically verified test cases across 9 batches. Each rule corresponds to an observed Radar output, not an assumption. When you find a mismatch, log it — every new case helps refine the model further.
